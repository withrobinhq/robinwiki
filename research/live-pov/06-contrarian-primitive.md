# 06 — The Wrong-Primitive Contrarian: Robin's atom should be the *question*, not the *statement*

## The reframed primitive: the **standing question** (an open inquiry with a current best answer)

Robin's atomic unit should not be a frozen statement. It should be a **standing question** — a thing a person keeps returning to ("Should I move to Lisbon?", "Is seed-stage worth it?", "Does caffeine actually help my focus?") — and a belief is merely that question's *current best answer*, a derived view, not a stored object. The question is the durable identity; the answer is a fold over evidence at a moment in time. Under this primitive a "fragment" is demoted to what it always actually was — a piece of *evidence that bears on some question* — and a "POV" stops being a special construct that needs liveness bolted onto it, because **liveness is the question's native state of being**. A question is live by definition: an unanswered or provisionally-answered question is the only kind there is. You don't ask "does this fragment change?" You ask "what's the best answer to this question, given everything that's arrived?" — and you recompute it whenever you look. The atom *is* the open loop, not the closed one.

## Why the current atom (the statement) is what makes liveness so awkward

The thesis is doing heroic engineering to make a *static text row* behave like a *living belief*, and every hard problem it lists is the scar tissue of that mismatch:

- **It froze the answer and lost the question.** A "fact"/"idea"/"decision" fragment records a conclusion but discards the inquiry that produced it. So when the world changes, there's nothing on the fragment that *wants* to be re-evaluated — a statement has no appetite for revision. The thesis has to invent a "stance" that "keeps asking 'does this change me?'" — i.e. it's *reconstructing the question* it threw away at atomization time. The POV is a prosthetic question grafted back onto a graveyard of answers.

- **Immutability is pinned to the wrong thing.** Making *text* immutable is easy and nearly worthless; making *the right answer* immutable would be false (beliefs should move). So the thesis splits the difference — text fixed, conviction floating — and now owns a two-headed object whose two heads can disagree. That's the source of the "facade" awkwardness: a POV's prose can say one thing while its conviction says another, and someone has to reconcile them. The real immutable thing is neither: it's **the event of evidence arriving**. Text and conviction should both be projections.

- **Cardinality is undefined because statements don't know what they're for.** "~3–9 push/pull fragments per POV" is a guess, and "which stance does this fragment attach to?" has no principled answer, *because a statement isn't intrinsically about anything*. "Lisbon has 300 sunny days" is For one person's relocation question, Against another's "I need seasonal variety" question, and irrelevant to a third. Relevance lives in the **question→evidence edge**, not in the fragment. Statement-as-atom forces you to pick one home for a homeless fact.

- **"Refuses to rot" is unachievable when the unit is an answer.** Answers rot the instant the world moves. Questions don't rot — they get re-answered. By choosing the answer as the atom, the thesis guaranteed the exact decay it's promising to prevent, then bolted on machinery to fight its own foundation.

## How the standing-question primitive *dissolves* (not solves) the open problems

- **Cardinality vanishes.** There's no target count of supporting fragments, because a question simply has *whatever evidence has been linked to it* — zero, three, or four hundred. "3–9" was an attempt to make a static object feel alive by capping it; a question needs no cap. Few links → low-confidence answer, surfaced as such. Many links → a richer fold. The number is an output, never a design constraint.

- **Attach/detach vanishes.** You never "attach a fragment to a POV." Evidence is linked to a **question** via a typed edge (`bears-on: for | against | reframes`), and the *same* piece of evidence can bear on many questions with different polarities simultaneously. No orphans, no forced single home, no migration when a fragment's relevance shifts — you add or retire an edge. The hard "which stance owns this?" problem evaporates because nothing owns anything; edges are many-to-many by construction.

- **Liveness cost vanishes as a special feature.** The thesis pays a recurring cost: every "live" POV must actively re-poll the corpus asking "does this change me?" — that's an O(live POVs × new evidence) background job, and it's the thing that's expensive and fragile. Reframed, **there is no live object to maintain.** The answer to a question is a *pure function of its linked evidence*, computed (or cached + invalidated) only when someone reads the question or when a new edge touches it. Liveness becomes lazy and demand-driven instead of eager and ambient. You don't run a daemon that nags every belief; you recompute a fold when its inputs change. Same UX ("conviction moved"), a fraction of the cost, and no possibility of a "live" object silently going stale because its poller missed something — the fold is always derived fresh from edges.

- **The event stream becomes the one true immutable.** Make the **append-only log of "evidence E arrived at time T, linked to question Q with polarity P"** the only immutable thing. Fragments, conviction, POV prose, and the wiki page are all *projections/folds over that log.* Supersession isn't a special operation — it's just a later event that down-weights or retires an edge, with full history preserved because you never mutate the log. "Show me what I believed last March" becomes *replay the fold to time T* — trivially, because the answer was never stored, only ever derived. The thesis's "superseded-not-deleted" rule is reinvented for free as the natural behavior of an append-only event store.

## The obvious objection, steelmanned — then answered

**Steelman:** "Statements are the right atom because they're what humans actually *capture*. People don't speak in questions — they dump assertions: 'Lisbon's great,' 'the API is rate-limited,' 'I should quit.' Forcing every capture through a question is unnatural friction, the LLM will hallucinate questions that the user never holds, and you'll get a brittle taxonomy of fake inquiries. Worse, much knowledge is *just reference* — facts and quotes with no live question attached ('Postgres default port is 5432'). A question-centric store mis-models the large inert part of a second brain and adds ceremony to the common case. Robin's whole pitch is *capture raw thoughts without manual organization* — questions reintroduce the organization tax."

**Answer:** The objection conflates the *capture surface* with the *storage primitive*, which is exactly the mistake the original thesis makes in reverse. Capture stays a raw assertion dump — nothing changes for the user; they still just talk. The question is **derived during the same background structuring pass that already extracts fragments and clusters threads** — Robin is *already* running an inference step, so inferring "this assertion bears on the standing question 'should I move to Lisbon?'" is the same class of work as "this fragment belongs in this thread cluster," not new ceremony. Inert reference facts are handled cleanly, not awkwardly: a fact with no question linked is just **unlinked evidence** — it sits in the corpus, fully searchable, costing nothing, *and never falsely presented as a "live POV."* That's strictly better than the thesis, which must decide whether '5432' is a fragment that should be polled for liveness (waste) or specially flagged as inert (a second category to maintain). Under questions, inert-vs-live isn't a property you tag — it's simply whether any open question currently points at the evidence. And on hallucination: a wrongly-inferred question is *cheap and reversible* (retire an edge), whereas a wrongly-frozen answer in the thesis is *load-bearing and sticky* — it becomes a POV facade that people read and trust. Getting the question slightly wrong degrades gracefully; getting the answer wrong rots silently. The friction the objection fears is real only if you make users author questions; you don't — you let the corpus's recurring assertions *reveal* the questions, which is the most Robin-native move there is.

## Verdict block
**VERDICT:** Robin's atomic unit should be the *standing question* (durable identity) with beliefs as folds over an immutable evidence-edge log — which makes liveness native and deletes the POV-vs-fragment-vs-wiki liveness fight entirely.
**CONVICTION:** high — every "hard problem" in the thesis (cardinality, attach/detach, liveness cost, anti-rot) is a direct symptom of atomizing the *answer* instead of the *question*, and each one dissolves rather than shrinks under the reframe; that one-to-one mapping between their pain and my diagnosis is hard to get by coincidence.
**Strongest argument:**
- The thesis's "stance that keeps asking 'does this change me?'" is literally a reconstructed question — it threw the question away at atomization, then rebuilt it as expensive prosthetic machinery.
- Cardinality and attach/detach are undefined *only because a statement isn't intrinsically about anything*; relevance lives on the question→evidence edge, so making the edge primary makes both problems vanish, not shrink.
- Liveness as a *fold over an append-only event log* is lazy, demand-driven, and always-fresh — versus the thesis's eager background poller that costs O(live × evidence) and can silently miss updates.
**What would change my mind:**
- Evidence that real users' captures are overwhelmingly *inert reference* with almost no recurring questions — then question-as-atom mis-models the bulk of the store and statement-as-atom is the honest default.
- A demonstration that reliably *deriving* the right standing question from raw assertions is materially harder/less accurate than extracting fragments — i.e. the inference cost lands on the expensive side, not the same side, as thread-clustering.
- A workload where conviction must update in true real-time at write (eager) and lazy fold-on-read introduces unacceptable latency, making an always-live object genuinely necessary rather than wasteful.
